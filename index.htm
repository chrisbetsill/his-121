Interestingly, it was comissioned by a European, the Norman king Roger II of Sicily. Thus, it represents both the Christian and Muslim undetstanding of the setting which frames their conflict. One common misconception of the Middle Ages is that it was a dark time of violence and barbarism.

Although there are problems calling the Middle Ages a “dark time” (there was plenty of intellectual progress), it is true that the time was undeniably violent. Lords, kings, emperors, and Sultans waged wars for territory, defense, religion, and power. It is a difficult and near impossible task to summarize warfare in the Middle Ages in a succinct way, given the wide range of actors and equipment that were used in the time. Instead of attempting to cover warfare throughout every distinct part the Middle Ages, we have focused on some of the basic aspects of warfare such as: tactics, recruiting, Holy War, and financing. The website compares how these aspects played out in Christian and Muslim contexts and how the two borrowed things from each other. In a narrower focus, this website has chosen to highlight the Third Crusade for comparison purposes. This is because two of the most prominent figures of the Third Crusade: Saladin and Richard I offer an excellent opportunity for comparison. The hope is to use the two figures to further flesh out how warfare was waged in the middle ages. They will also provide insight into the differences between Christian and Islam holy war and whether there were any underlying differences in how the two religions waged war in general. Saladin and Richard I are unable to encompass everything one should know about warfare in the Middle Ages, but they provide much of the essential knowledge and represent interesting case studies. The goal of this website is to provide knowledge of warfare in the Middle Ages, Saladin, Richard I, and the Third Crusade concisely.
